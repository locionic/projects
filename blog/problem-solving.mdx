# Problem Solving Skills

Problems happen. The difference between someone who gets stuck and someone who moves forward often comes down to how they approach the problem.

Good problem solvers don't just react. They have a process.

## What Good Problem Solving Looks Like

Think of problem solving as a skill you can build, not a talent you're born with.

The best problem solvers share a few traits:

- They dig into the root cause instead of treating symptoms
- They consider multiple solutions before committing to one
- They stay flexible and adapt as they learn more

Here's a practical framework you can use.

## Key Takeaways

- Start by understanding the real problem, not just what you see on the surface
- Generate many solutions before evaluating any of them
- Choose based on fit, not just what's quickest
- Build feedback loops into your implementation
- Update your solution as things change

## Step 1: Define the Problem

**Answer:** You fix what you understand. So slow down and diagnose first.

Before jumping to solutions, map out the situation:

What actually happens? Who does what, with what tools, talking to whom? When does it go wrong?

Document the current process. Use a flow chart if it helps. The goal is to see the steps clearly.

Then look for root causes, not symptoms. A root cause is the "why" behind the problem. Fixing symptoms just makes the problem come back in a different outfit.

## Step 2: Generate Alternative Solutions

**Answer:** Come up with several options before you pick any.

This is where most people rush. They hear one idea and grab it. But the first workable solution is rarely the best one.

Brainstorm freely at first. Don't evaluate yet. Write down everything, even stuff that sounds weird. The goal is quantity, not quality.

The mistake to avoid: evaluating solutions as you think of them. When you pair ideation with judgment, you kill creativity before it gets started.

## Step 3: Evaluate and Select an Alternative

**Answer:** Judge options against your actual needs.

Now you evaluate. For each option, ask:

- Does this actually solve the problem without creating new ones?
- Will the people involved actually accept it?
- Is it realistic to implement?
- Does it fit within your constraints?

Pick the option that best fits your situation. Not the fastest. Not the simplest. The one that actually works.

## Step 4: Implement and Follow Up

**Answer:** Roll out the solution and watch what happens.

Tell people about it. Get buy-in early. The more people feel involved, the less resistance you'll hit.

But don't just launch and leave. Build in feedback channels. Monitor what's actually happening against what you expected.

A solution that worked last month might not work next year. Stay curious. Adjust as needed.

## FAQ

**How do I find the root cause of a problem?**

Ask "why" five times. Each answer becomes the next question. Keep going until you hit something actionable. Also try cause-and-effect diagrams to map out all the factors at play.

**How many solutions should I generate before picking one?**

There's no magic number. But three is usually too few and twenty is overkill. Aim for enough options to give yourself real choice, then evaluate.

**What if my team doesn't like the solution I chose?**

Get them involved earlier next time. If the solution is already picked, explain the reasoning. Listen to concerns. Sometimes adjusting the implementation fixes the pushback without changing the solution itself.

**How do I know if the solution actually worked?**

Set up checkpoints. Monitor the metrics that matter. Compare actual results to what you expected. If something's off, dig back in.

**Is problem solving a natural talent or a learned skill?**

It's learned. The frameworks and habits around problem solving are skills like any other. Practice them and you'll get better at it.
