# The Rise of Agentic Programming: Opportunities and Challenges

Last week, I attended The Orchard Retreat, a gathering of software developers discussing the future of the profession in light of the rise of agentic programming. The event was held under the Chatham House Rule, so I won't attribute comments and stories to specific individuals. However, I'd like to share some key takeaways from the discussions.

## Porting Code with LLMs

One group shared their experience of developing a behavioral clone of the GNU Cobol compiler in Rust, using LLMs to port the existing code. The result was 70,000 lines of Rust code built in just three days. This achievement highlights the potential of LLMs in porting existing code to new platforms. However, it also emphasizes the importance of good regression tests and the need for a test suite to ensure the correctness of the ported code.

## Interrogatory LLMs

Another attendee shared an innovative approach to reviewing large spec documents using LLMs. By having the LLM interview a human expert, asking questions to verify the correctness of the specification, they created a form of Interrogatory LLM. This approach can help reduce the complexity of reviewing large documents and improve the accuracy of the review process.

## Understanding Organizational History

One attendee shared a valuable insight into understanding organizational history by reading the guidelines for the change-control board. This approach helps tap into the scar tissue of what's gone wrong in the past and provides valuable context for making informed decisions.

## Reevaluating "Lift and Shift"

The traditional approach to modernizing legacy systems, known as "Lift and Shift," involves porting a legacy system to a new platform while retaining feature parity. However, with the rise of LLMs, this approach may need to be reevaluated. One attendee suggested that lifting and shifting to a new platform should be the first step in a legacy migration, as the cost is no longer as formidable as it used to be. This approach can make further evolution much cheaper, but it's essential to take a step back and understand what users currently need, prioritizing those needs against business outcomes and metrics.

## Simplifying Complex Legacy Environments

Attendees from the financial industry shared their experiences with complex legacy environments, regulatory controls, and significant risk. They discussed the potential of using LLMs to build individual, simpler systems for each jurisdiction, ensuring consistency between them, and using LLMs to ensure that product rules changes are reflected in each system.

## Teaching Judgment in Software Development

The group discussed the importance of teaching junior developers good judgment in software development, particularly when working with LLMs. Pair programming was suggested as a valuable tool for skills transfer, allowing experienced developers to pass on their judgment for software design and how to use LLMs effectively.

## Chaos Engineering for AI

The concept of Chaos Engineering was discussed in the context of AI, with the idea of introducing hallucinations into a pipeline to see if sensors can catch them. This approach can help improve the resiliency of AI systems and identify potential weaknesses.

## The Role of LLMs in Review Processes

A Q&A session on Structured-Prompt-Driven Development (SPDD) highlighted the potential of using LLMs to review prompts and specifications. However, the authors noted that there are downsides to automating this process, including the need for humans to learn from the AI's choices and the potential for blocking long-term skill growth.

## The Importance of Ergonomics in Software Development

A personal anecdote about injuring my elbow while working on a computer highlighted the importance of ergonomics in software development. The use of voice input was suggested as a potential solution, but the back-and-forth between writing and editing code makes this approach challenging.

## The Misuse of Agents in Software Development

James Pritchard's thoughts on the misuse of agents in software development were shared, highlighting the problem of using agents when functions would be more suitable. Agents can work unpredictably, making debugging more challenging, and functions compose predictably, making them a better choice for many use cases.

## The Overuse of Skills in LLMs

Pritchard also discussed the overuse of skills in LLMs, suggesting that many things that should go in skills should be part of a harness, preferably computational. Skills should only be used with deliberate, infrequent workflows, and the skills obsession is a symptom of a deeper pattern: people reaching for configuration when they should be reaching for architecture.

## The Future of Software Development

Kyle Kingsbury's article on the future of software development was discussed, highlighting the need to deal with non-determinism in our work. Kingsbury's view is that the best response to the rise of LLMs is to stop using them, citing concerns about their impact on the shape of cities and the potential for chaos.

Overall, the discussions at The Orchard Retreat highlighted the opportunities and challenges presented by the rise of agentic programming. As software developers, we need to adapt to non-determinism, reevaluate our testing and security approaches, and consider the role of LLMs in the development process.
