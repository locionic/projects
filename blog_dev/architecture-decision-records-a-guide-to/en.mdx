# Architecture Decision Records: A Guide to Documenting Software Architecture Decisions

An Architecture Decision Record (ADR) is a brief document that captures a single decision relevant to a product or ecosystem. The primary purpose of an ADR is to provide a clear understanding of the decision, its context, and its consequences. This document serves as a record of the decision-making process, allowing team members and stakeholders to understand the reasoning behind the decision.

## Purpose of ADRs

ADRs serve two primary purposes:

1.  **Record of decisions**: ADRs provide a historical record of decisions made during the development process, allowing team members to understand why certain design choices were made.
2.  **Clarifying thinking**: The process of writing an ADR helps to clarify the decision-making process, particularly when working in groups. It forces team members to discuss and resolve differences in opinion.

## Structure of an ADR

An ADR should follow an "inverted pyramid" style of writing, with the most important information presented first. The document should include:

*   **Decision**: A clear statement of the decision made.
*   **Context**: The circumstances that led to the decision.
*   **Rationale**: A brief explanation of the reasoning behind the decision, including any trade-offs considered.
*   **Alternatives**: A list of serious alternatives that were considered, along with their pros and cons.
*   **Consequences**: A clear statement of the consequences of the decision.
*   **Confidence level**: An assessment of the confidence level in the decision.
*   **Reevaluation triggers**: Any changes in the product context that should trigger a reevaluation of the decision.

## Best Practices for ADRs

*   **Keep it concise**: ADRs should be brief, typically no more than a single page.
*   **Use a lightweight markup language**: ADRs should be written in a lightweight markup language, such as Markdown, to make them easy to read and diff.
*   **Store in the code repository**: ADRs should be stored in the code repository, in a directory such as `doc/adr`.
*   **Use a monotonic sequence**: Each ADR should be numbered in a monotonic sequence as part of its file name.
*   **Include a status**: Each ADR should have a status, such as "proposed," "accepted," or "superseded."

## Example of an ADR

```markdown
# 0001-HTMX-for-active-web-pages

## Decision

We will use HTMX to implement active web pages in our application.

## Context

We need to implement active web pages in our application to improve user engagement. After researching various options, we decided to use HTMX due to its simplicity and ease of use.

## Rationale

HTMX provides a simple and efficient way to implement active web pages. It is also well-documented and has a large community of developers who contribute to it.

## Alternatives

We considered using React or Angular, but they were too complex and heavy for our needs. We also considered using a custom solution, but it would have required too much development time.

## Consequences

Using HTMX will allow us to implement active web pages quickly and efficiently. It will also make it easier to maintain and update our application in the future.

## Confidence level

We are confident that HTMX is the right choice for our application, based on our research and testing.

## Reevaluation triggers

If we encounter any significant issues with HTMX, we will reevaluate our decision and consider alternative solutions.
```
