# Slack AI: The Path to Multi-Cloud

In early 2023, Slack faced a significant challenge: serving Large Language Models (LLMs) at enterprise scale while meeting the security, reliability, and performance expectations of its customers. Over the course of three years, Slack's architecture evolved from basic infrastructure to a sophisticated multi-cloud architecture, ensuring resilience to regional outages and GPU scarcity.

## Phase 1: The SageMaker Era

Slack's initial foray into AI infrastructure began with AWS SageMaker, a managed ML Serving platform that offered security, FedRamp compliance, model availability, and control. To maximize uptime for a global user base, Slack deployed containers across multiple AWS regions, managing operational lifecycles, including cross-region IAM roles, balanced routing, proactive capacity planning, and auto-scaling logic.

However, this approach came with significant overhead, including:

* **Scaling Latency**: Initialization times prevented instantaneous scaling.
* **Hardware Scarcity**: Enterprise-grade Nvidia GPUs were often unavailable.
* **Over-Provisioning**: Maintaining idle resources to meet peak SLAs.

By early 2024, Slack mitigated these issues through On-Demand Capacity Reservations (ODCR) and proactive, cron-based scaling. However, this reinforced the need for automated capacity, rather than manual coordination.

## Phase 2: Migrating to Amazon Bedrock for Agility and Access

By mid-2024, AWS Bedrock had matured, offering FedRamp Moderate compliance and a similar security posture to SageMaker. Slack's decision to migrate to Bedrock provided three immediate advantages:

* **Operational Simplicity**: Fully managed AWS service, eliminating the need for individual GPU instance scaling.
* **Immediate Model Access**: Eliminated LLM model feature lag, with access to the latest models shortly after public availability.
* **Infrastructure Efficiency**: Introduced Provisioned Throughput (PT) and On Demand (OD) infrastructure options, allowing Slack to tailor compute to specific use cases.

Slack utilized PT for interactive, latency-sensitive features and OD for bursty, scheduled workloads, eliminating costs for idle compute. The migration was executed through a multi-stage strategy, ensuring zero customer-facing incidents.

## Phase 3: Transitioning to Bedrock On-Demand

With high confidence in Bedrock and mature monitoring, Slack moved to close the final efficiency and quality gap. Historical analysis revealed feature usage fluctuations, leaving idle capacity overnight. Moving to on-demand infrastructure allowed Slack to solve the idle capacity problem, providing architectural agility to support highly variable workloads without manual over-provisioning.

## Phase 4: Hybrid Strategy and Multi-Cloud Architecture

Slack implemented a Hybrid Routing strategy, balancing efficiency with a premium user experience. High-volume, latency-sensitive features remained on dedicated capacity (Provisioned Throughput), while asynchronous, bursty workloads moved to On-Demand capacity.

To bridge the gap, Slack engineered a Spillover Pattern, ensuring excess requests automatically spilled over to on-demand endpoints, preventing capacity ceilings. This approach introduced new variables, including service level variability, regional capacity orchestration, and concentration risk.

To mitigate these risks, Slack developed a more intelligent AI Platform abstraction, including a model hierarchy for every AI feature, allowing the system to automatically fall back to different models if the primary model reached a degraded state.

## Conclusion

Slack's journey to a multi-cloud architecture for serving LLMs at enterprise scale has been marked by significant challenges and opportunities. By embracing a hybrid strategy and investing in a more intelligent AI Platform abstraction, Slack has ensured the security, reliability, and performance of its AI features, while maintaining the agility and flexibility required to stay ahead in the rapidly evolving AI landscape.
