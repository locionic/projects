# The Virtue of Laziness in Software Development

I recently attended the Pragmatic Summit, where I had the opportunity to discuss the future of software development with Kent Beck and Gergely Orosz. One topic that dominated our conversation was the rise of Artificial Intelligence (AI) and its potential impact on the industry.

## The Three Virtues of a Programmer

In his book on Perl, Larry Wall highlights the three virtues of a programmer: hubris, impatience, and laziness. Bryan Cantrill, a well-known programmer, has also praised the virtue of laziness, arguing that it drives us to create simple and powerful abstractions that make our work easier.

## The Limitations of Large Language Models (LLMs)

While LLMs are incredibly good at writing code, they lack the virtue of laziness. They do not feel the need to optimize for their own time or the time of others, and will happily produce large amounts of code without considering the consequences. This can lead to systems that are larger, but not better.

## The Importance of Human Laziness

Human laziness is essential in software development because it forces us to develop crisp abstractions and optimize for our own time. This constraint drives us to make systems simpler, despite their essential complexity. The best engineering is always borne of constraints, and the constraint of our time places limits on the cognitive load of the system that we're willing to accept.

## A Personal Example

I recently spent some time modifying my music playlist generator, and initially, I was doing it in a more complicated way than necessary. However, by applying the principle of "You Ain't Gonna Need It" (YAGNI), I was able to simplify the system and reduce the amount of code. If I had used an LLM for this task, it may have produced a more complicated solution that would have caused problems in the future.

## Applying Test-Driven Development to AI Systems

Jessica Kerr has demonstrated how to apply the principle of Test-Driven Development (TDD) to prompting agents. By instructing the agent to look for documentation files and update them, and then verifying that the updates are correct, we can ensure that our AI systems are producing high-quality code.

## The Importance of Doubt in AI Systems

Mark Little has highlighted the importance of doubt in AI systems, citing the classic SciFi movie "Dark Star" as an example. In the movie, a sentient bomb is prevented from detonating by a crew member who uses philosophical argument to make the bomb doubt its sensors. This is a useful metaphor for where we are with AI today, as most AI systems are optimized for decisiveness rather than doubt.

## Designing AI Systems with Restraint

In order to create AI systems that can operate safely without constant human oversight, we need to teach them not just how to decide, but when not to. This means designing systems that can doubt and defer, rather than simply producing an output based on probabilistic inference. Restraint is not a limitation, but a capability that is essential in many cases.

```python
# Example code demonstrating the principle of YAGNI
def generate_playlist(songs):
    # Simplified implementation
    return [song for song in songs if song.genre == 'rock']

# Example code demonstrating the principle of TDD
def test_generate_playlist():
    # Test that the playlist generator produces the correct output
    songs = ['song1', 'song2', 'song3']
    expected_output = ['song1', 'song3']
    assert generate_playlist(songs) == expected_output

test_generate_playlist()
```

In conclusion, human laziness is a virtue that drives us to create simple and powerful abstractions in software development. While LLMs are incredibly good at writing code, they lack this virtue and can produce systems that are larger, but not better. By designing AI systems with doubt and restraint, we can create systems that are safer and more reliable.
