# The Python Libraries That Actually Deliver

I've lost count of how many weekends I've wasted on the wrong library. Pick the shiny new thing, build for three hours, then realize it's got a showstopper bug or zero community support. Not doing that again.

Here are the tools I'm actually betting my projects on in 2026. No hype, just stuff that works when you ship.

<Callout type="idea" title="The Rule">
"It works" is not the same as "it works when you ship." Pick libraries that scale with your sanity.
</Callout>

---

## Web and APIs

<Stepper>
  <Step title="FastAPI (The Default)">
    This is my go-to for anything API-shaped. It's fast, the type validation via Pydantic catches bugs before they happen, and it auto-generates Swagger docs. I can throw together an API endpoint in minutes and know it won't blow up at 3 AM.

## Data and Machine Learning

<Callout type="info" title="Data Manipulation">

**pandas:** Still king for CSV and Excel work. It's not the fastest, but it's everywhere and everyone knows it.

**polars:** When pandas chokes on memory, I switch to Polars. It's blisteringly fast and handles datasets that would bring pandas to its knees.

</Callout>

<Callout type="info" title="Machine Learning and AI">

**scikit-learn:** This is the backbone for classic ML. Regression, clustering, random forests — it's boring, reliable, and I trust it.

**PyTorch:** For deep learning, it's not even a contest. PyTorch won the framework war. The research community lives here, and so should you.

**Hugging Face Transformers:** Want to run or fine-tune an LLM? Start here. It's the default for good reason — thousands of pre-trained models, one consistent API.

</Callout>

## Testing and Tooling

<Stepper>
  <Step title="Testing: pytest">
    Just use pytest. Seriously. Fixtures, parametrization, plug-ins — it's the undisputed king for a reason. I haven't touched unittest in years.
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Linting: ruff">
    Ruff replaces Flake8, Black, isort, and half a dozen other tools. It's insanely fast. Like, blink-and-you'll-miss-it fast. It ended every formatting argument on my team overnight.
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Package Management: Poetry">
    Broken requirements.txt files used to drive me crazy. Poetry gives you sane dependency management with lock files, just like you'd expect from any modern ecosystem. I should have switched sooner.
  </Step>
</Stepper>

<Callout type="warning" title="A Note on Async">
Don't reach for async (aiohttp, httpx) unless you really need high concurrency. For 90% of scripts, plain old requests is cleaner, more readable, and completely fine. I learned this one the hard way.
</Callout>

## The Honest Truth

Here's what nobody tells you: the best library is the one you already know. Every tool on this list is great, but they won't save you from bad architecture or unclear requirements.

Pick something boring. Ship it. Iterate. That pattern has never failed me. The library you choose matters a lot less than whether you actually finish the project.
