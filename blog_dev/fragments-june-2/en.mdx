Greg Wilson has pointed out that many organizations are using flawed metrics to measure the effectiveness of AI tools. These metrics include lines of code generated, tickets closed, and surveys asking developers if they feel more productive. However, these approaches are problematic, and Wilson argues that we need better ways to measure productivity.

Martin Fowler acknowledges that measuring productivity is challenging, but suggests that even imperfect metrics can be useful. He uses a flawed measure, "Asking Developers If They Feel More Productive," but acknowledges its limitations.

## The Impact of Automation on Jobs
------------------------------------

Benedict Evans notes that automation has not led to the demise of professions in the past. For example, the automation of accounting has led to an increase in the number of accountants. Evans argues that forecasting the impact of AI on jobs is difficult due to the Jevons paradox, which states that increased efficiency can lead to increased demand.

## The Performance of Open and Closed Models
---------------------------------------------

Stephen O'Grady has analyzed the performance of open and closed models on benchmarks over time. He finds that closed models are setting the pace of innovation, while open models are chasing them. However, the cycle times for open models to catch up are getting shorter.

## The Dangers of Hallucinated Citations
------------------------------------------

GPTZero, a company that makes tools to detect AI writing, has published investigations highlighting the problem of hallucinated citations. These are citations that are not based on actual sources but are instead generated by AI models. Hallucinated citations can "poison the well" by misleading future researchers and perpetuating false information.

## The Use of AI for Defense
-----------------------------

Mozilla has used AI models to identify and fix security bugs in Firefox. This is a positive example of how AI can be used for defense, rather than just offense.

## The Multiplication of Technical Debt
------------------------------------------

Pavel Voronin notes that technical debt in a codebase can be multiplied by LLMs. These models can learn from existing code and perpetuate bad practices. Voronin introduces the concept of "cognitive debt" and "generative debt," which refer to the accumulation of technical debt due to the use of abstractions that are no longer understood and the perpetuation of confused concepts.

## The Rise of "Humanizers"
-----------------------------

Jason Koebler has written about the rise of "humanizers," AI tools that make writing look less like AI. These tools can add typos, replace words, and remove "AI tells." However, Koebler argues that this is a step towards the "Zombie internet," where humans are no longer able to distinguish between human and AI-generated content.

## The Importance of Designing Workflow with AI Agents
-------------------------------------------------

Andy Osmani points out that spawning lots of AI agents is like launching parallel processes that rely on a single orchestrating thread. He argues that designers need to consider the limitations of human attention and design workflows that take into account the need for human verification.

## The Gains and Losses of Using LLMs
------------------------------------------

Jamie Hurst, a Principal Engineer at Booking.com, has written about the gains and losses of using LLMs in software development. He notes that the cost of building has collapsed, but the cost of aligning organizationally has not. Hurst argues that the bottleneck has moved from engineering to coordination and that the productivity gains from AI have been captured by output volume rather than output quality.
