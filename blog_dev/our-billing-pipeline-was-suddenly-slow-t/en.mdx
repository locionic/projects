We use ClickHouse to store over a hundred petabytes of data across a few dozen clusters. Our Ready-Analytics system allows teams to stream data into a single, massive table, disambiguating datasets by namespace and using a standard schema. The primary key is composed of (namespace, indexID, timestamp), allowing for optimal sorting and querying.

## The Problem: One Retention Policy to Rule Them All
----------------------------------------------------

Our retention policy was a major limitation, with a "one-size-fits-all" 31-day retention period. We needed a new system that allowed per-namespace retention.

## The Solution: A New Partitioning Scheme
------------------------------------------

We changed the partitioning key from (day) to (namespace, day), allowing our existing retention system to continue managing partitions with per-namespace granularity. We assumed this wouldn't affect performance since every query is filtered by a specific namespace.

## The Mystery: When Billing Starts to Break
--------------------------------------------

Two months after the migration, our billing team reported that their daily aggregation jobs were slowing down. We investigated, but none of the usual suspects (I/O, memory, rows scanned, parts read) were to blame. It took several days before we even had a theory.

## The Investigation: Hunting Bottlenecks with Flame Graphs
---------------------------------------------------------

We used ClickHouse's built-in trace_log to generate flame graphs. The first CPU-based flame graph showed that a huge amount of time was being spent in query planning. We then switched to "Real" traces, which sample all threads, including those that are inactive or waiting. The new flame graph revealed massive lock contention.

## The Fixes: A Trio of Patches
-------------------------------

### Optimization 1: Use a Shared Lock

We modified the code to acquire a shared lock instead of an exclusive lock. This allowed all query planners to enter the critical section concurrently.

### Optimization 2: Stop Copying the Vector

We deferred the copy of the parts list, creating a "shared copy" that read-only operations can access. Any operation that modifies the set of parts regenerates the cache.

### Optimization 3: Binary Search for Parts

We implemented a binary search based on the namespace part of the partition ID. This works because the vector is sorted, allowing us to filter out a lot of entries without actually looking at them.

## Conclusion
----------

These optimizations resolved the immediate crisis with the billing system. However, this journey exposed the deep, non-obvious costs of our partitioning choice. Other problems remain, and we continue to explore more generic approaches to optimize query planning and part filtering.
