If you've ever stared at your screen for three minutes after typing your password, waiting for Ubuntu to finally show your desktop, you're not alone. Here's a real-world debugging journey on fixing this agonizing delay caused by NVIDIA DRM modeset conflicts.

<Callout type="info" title="The Symptoms">
- System boot time is perfectly normal (~20 seconds)
- Xorg session (GNOME desktop) with an NVIDIA GPU
- **The Issue:** A solid 2-3 minute total freeze right after logging in!
</Callout>

---

## The Investigation

My first thought was system services slowing down the boot. I ran `systemd-analyze` and `systemd-analyze --user blame`, but found nothing out of the ordinary. 

<Callout type="idea" title="The Breakthrough Clue">
The real clue was hiding in the kernel logs! I found repeated errors:
`[nvidia-drm] Failed to grab modeset ownership` and `nvidia-gpu ... i2c timeout error`
</Callout>

## The Root Cause

The issue boiled down to an **NVIDIA DRM/KMS modesetting conflict** during the Xorg startup process. 

<Stepper>
  <Step title="The Power Struggle">
    `nvidia-drm` tried to take control of the display output, but another process already held the DRM ownership.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The Waiting Game">
    The driver kept aggressively retrying for 2-3 minutes, locking up the session.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The Surrender">
    Eventually, it timed out, released control, and the GNOME session was finally allowed to load.
  </Step>
</Stepper>

---

## The Fix

The solution is remarkably simple: **Disable NVIDIA DRM modesetting.**

<Stepper>
  <Step title="Open GRUB Config">
    Open your terminal and edit the GRUB configuration file:
    ```bash
    sudo nano /etc/default/grub
    ```
  </Step>
  <Step title="Update Boot Parameters">
    Find `GRUB_CMDLINE_LINUX_DEFAULT` and add `nvidia-drm.modeset=0`:
    ```text
    GRUB_CMDLINE_LINUX_DEFAULT="quiet splash nvidia-drm.modeset=0"
    ```
  </Step>
  <Step title="Apply & Reboot">
    Apply the new configuration and restart your machine:
    ```bash
    sudo update-grub && sudo reboot
    ```
  </Step>
</Stepper>

<Callout type="warning" title="Why This Works">
Disabling DRM modesetting stops the deadlock entirely, allowing the boot sequence to bypass the NVIDIA DRM ownership timeout and go straight from the Kernel to the NVIDIA driver to Xorg. 
</Callout>

After this tweak, the desktop appeared instantly upon login. Smooth sailing!
