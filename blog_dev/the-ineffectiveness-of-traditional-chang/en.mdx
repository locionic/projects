# The Ineffectiveness of Traditional Change Controls: Lessons from the Swedbank Outage

The recent judgment from the Swedish FSA on the Swedbank outage has shed light on the limitations of traditional change controls in managing IT risk. In April 2022, Swedbank experienced a major outage caused by an unapproved change to their IT systems, resulting in incorrect balances for nearly a million customers. The regulator's investigation found that Swedbank had not followed their change management process, leading to a SEK850M (~85M USD) fine.

## What Went Wrong?

The judgment highlights the deficiencies in Swedbank's internal control, which allowed changes to be made to their central IT systems without following the established process. The regulator noted that none of the bank's control mechanisms were able to capture the deviation and ensure that the process was followed. The incident was attributed to non-compliance with the change management process, which likely contributed to a slower analysis of the incident and a greater impact on operations.

## The Limitations of Traditional Change Controls

The Swedbank incident is not an isolated case. Research has shown that traditional change controls, such as Change Advisory Boards (CABs), are often ineffective in managing IT risk. A study by the UK Financial Conduct Authority found that CABs approved over 90% of major changes, raising questions about their effectiveness as an assurance mechanism. Similarly, research by Dr. Nicole Forsgren, Jez Humble, and Gene Kim found that external approvals were negatively correlated with lead time, deployment frequency, and restore time, and had no correlation with change fail rate.

## Why Traditional Change Controls Don't Work

Traditional change controls focus on paperwork and process conformance, but they don't reduce risk in the way that's intended. They reduce the risk of undocumented changes, but risks in changes that are fully documented can still sail through the approval process unnoticed. This is because traditional change controls don't address the underlying risk of changes, but rather focus on checking boxes and following procedures.

## A New Approach to Change Management

So, what can be done to reduce IT risk? The answer lies in adopting a DevSecOps approach to change management, which harmonizes the speed of software delivery with the demands made on organizations by cybersecurity and audit and compliance. This approach involves:

* Automating change controls and documentation
* Introducing monitoring and alerting systems to detect unauthorized changes
* Focusing on reducing the risk of changes, rather than just checking boxes
* Implementing smaller, more frequent changes to reduce the likelihood and impact of change-related incidents

## Conclusion

The Swedbank outage highlights the need for a new approach to change management, one that focuses on reducing IT risk through automation, monitoring, and smaller, more frequent changes. Traditional change controls are no longer effective in managing IT risk, and it's time for organizations to adopt a more modern and effective approach to change management. By doing so, organizations can reduce the likelihood and impact of change-related incidents, and improve the overall security and reliability of their IT systems.

### Key Takeaways

* Traditional change controls are ineffective in managing IT risk
* A DevSecOps approach to change management can help reduce risk through automation, monitoring, and smaller, more frequent changes
* Focus on reducing the risk of changes, rather than just checking boxes
* Implement monitoring and alerting systems to detect unauthorized changes
* Automate change controls and documentation to reduce toil and improve efficiency
