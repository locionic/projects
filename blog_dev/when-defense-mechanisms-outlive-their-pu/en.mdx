# When Defense Mechanisms Outlive Their Purpose: A Lesson in Scalable Security

Building a robust defense system is crucial for maintaining a platform like GitHub. Rate limits, traffic controls, and protective measures across multiple infrastructure layers work together to keep the service healthy during abuse or attacks. However, these same protections can sometimes outlive their usefulness, causing unintended consequences and disrupting legitimate users.

## The Problem: Outdated Protections

We recently encountered an issue where users were experiencing "too many requests" errors during normal, low-volume browsing. Upon investigation, we discovered that protection rules added during past abuse incidents had been left in place, causing false positives and blocking legitimate requests.

## Investigating the Issue

To understand the root cause, we analyzed user reports, edge tier logs, application tier logs, and protection rule configurations. We found that composite signals, which combine industry-standard fingerprinting techniques with platform-specific business logic, were occasionally producing false positives. Although the overall impact was small, it still meant that real users were incorrectly blocked during normal browsing.

## The Challenge of Defending at Scale

During active incidents, responding quickly often means accepting tradeoffs to keep the service available. Emergency controls are necessary at the moment, but they don't age well as threat patterns evolve and legitimate tools and usage change. Without active maintenance, temporary mitigations become permanent, and their side effects compound quietly.

## Tracing Through the Stack

Our investigation highlighted the complexity of tracing requests across multiple layers of infrastructure. We use a custom, multi-layered protection infrastructure tailored to GitHub's unique operational requirements and scale. Each layer has legitimate reasons to rate-limit or block requests, but correlating logs across multiple systems with different schemas is a significant challenge.

## The Lifecycle of Incident Mitigations

We realized that incident mitigations require a lifecycle approach, including:

* Setting expiration dates for temporary controls
* Conducting post-incident rule reviews
* Monitoring impact and adjusting controls accordingly

By not consistently applying these practices, we accumulated technical debt that eventually affected users.

## What We Did

We reviewed and removed outdated mitigations, analyzing what each one was blocking today versus what it was meant to block when created. We kept protections against ongoing threats and improved our lifecycle management of protective controls.

## What We're Building

Beyond the immediate fix, we're working on:

* Better visibility across all protection layers to trace the source of rate limits and blocks
* Treating incident mitigations as temporary by default
* Making them permanent should require an intentional, documented decision
* Post-incident practices that evaluate emergency controls and evolve them into sustainable, targeted solutions

Defense mechanisms – even those deployed quickly during incidents – need the same care as the systems they protect. They require observability, documentation, and active maintenance to ensure they don't outlive their purpose.
