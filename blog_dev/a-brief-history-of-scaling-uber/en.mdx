# A Brief History of Scaling Uber
=====================================================

This post is a rewritten version of Josh Clemm's LinkedIn article, where he shares his experience as the Senior Director of Engineering at Uber Eats. We will explore the journey of scaling Uber from its early days to becoming the largest mobility platform in the world.

### The Idea Behind Uber

In 2008, Travis Kalanick and Garrett Camp couldn't get a cab in Paris. This led to the idea of creating a platform where users could "push a button and get a ride." Fast forward to today, Uber operates in over 70 countries and 10,500 cities, connecting millions of driver-partners and merchants with over 130 million customers.

### Early Days of Uber (2009)

The first version of Uber was built using the classic LAMP stack, with code written in Spanish. However, as the product gained popularity, the original architecture couldn't scale. Concurrency issues arose, such as dispatching two cars to one person or matching one driver to two different riders.

### Setting Up for Global Scale (2011)

To address these issues, Uber adopted Node.js for its real-time needs, becoming one of the first major adopters of Node.js in production. Node.js handled requests asynchronously, allowing for quick processing of significant amounts of data. A second service was built in Python to handle business logic functions like authentication, promotions, and fare calculation.

### The Two-Monolith Architecture

The resulting architecture consisted of two services: one built in Node.js ("dispatch") connected to MongoDB (later Redis), and the other built in Python ("API") connected to PostgreSQL. This architecture allowed for easier separation of engineers into dedicated teams, enabling more rapid team scaling.

### From Monolith to Microservices (2013)

As the team and number of features grew, the API service became too large, leading to conflicts and slow engineering productivity. To address this, Uber adopted a microservice architecture, breaking down the API into smaller services dedicated to specific domain areas. Each service could be written in its own language or framework, with its own database or lack thereof.

### Operational Complexity of Microservices

While microservices solved many problems, they also introduced significant operational complexity. Uber developed tools to counteract these tradeoffs, such as:

* Clay: a Python wrapper on Flask to build restful backend services
* TChannel: a bi-directional RPC protocol for service communication
* Apache Thrift: for well-defined RPC interfaces and stronger contracts across services
* Flipr: for feature flagging and config-based use cases
* M3: for observability of service metrics
* Nagios: for alerting at scale
* Merckx (later replaced by Jaeger): for distributed tracing across services

### Scaling the Trip Database (2014)

As Uber continued to grow, the single PostgreSQL database became a bottleneck. To address this, Uber developed Schemaless, a new trip data store built on top of MySQL. This allowed for horizontal scaling by partitioning rows across multiple shards, supporting rapid development culture.

### Splitting Up Dispatch (2014)

The original dispatch service was split into two areas of cleaner separation: a real-time API gateway (RTAPI) and an actual dispatch service. RTAPI provided a powerful new real-time layer, maintaining high development velocity. The dispatch service was rewritten into a series of services, understanding more about vehicle types and rider needs.

### Mobile App Development at Scale (2016-Present)

The flagship Uber product relied heavily on the new mobile paradigm created by the iPhone and Android OS launches. As Uber scaled globally, the mobile app's repositories faced similar bottlenecks to a backend monolith. Many features and engineers worked on the app, leading to the need for an ever-growing list of features specific to certain countries.

Uber's journey to becoming the largest mobility platform in the world was marked by significant architectural changes, from a monolithic architecture to a microservice architecture, and the development of new technologies to support its growth.
