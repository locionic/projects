# Recent Advances in AI-Assisted Programming

## Lattice Framework: Operationalizing AI-Assisted Programming Patterns

Rahul Garg has developed an open-source framework called Lattice to operationalize AI-assisted programming patterns. Lattice addresses the limitations of current AI coding assistants, which often make design decisions without considering the broader context. The framework consists of three tiers: atoms, molecules, and refiners, which embed battle-tested engineering disciplines such as Clean Architecture, DDD, and secure coding. A living context layer, the `.lattice/` folder, accumulates project standards, decisions, and review insights, allowing the system to learn and improve over time. Lattice can be installed as a Claude Code plugin or used with any AI tool.

## Structured-Prompt-Driven Development (SPDD)

Wei Zhang and Jessie Jie Xia's article on SPDD has generated significant interest, leading to the addition of a Q&A section. SPDD is a methodology that leverages structured prompts to drive software development. The Q&A section addresses common questions and provides further insights into the application of SPDD.

## The Double Feedback Loop in Agentic Programming

Jessica Kerr (Jessitron) has written about the double feedback loop involved in agentic programming. The first loop is the development loop, where the AI tool responds to user input, and the user checks the output. The second loop is the meta-level feedback loop, where the user evaluates the effectiveness of the tool and makes adjustments. Kerr notes that this double loop allows developers to mold their work environment and make changes to the tool itself, leading to a more efficient and enjoyable development process.

## The Importance of Internal Reprogramability

The concept of internal reprogramability, where developers can modify their development environment to fit their needs, is a key aspect of agentic programming. This idea was central to the Smalltalk and Lisp communities but was lost as IDEs became more complex. The rise of AI-assisted programming is bringing back the idea of internal reprogramability, allowing developers to tailor their environment to their specific needs.

## The Risks of AI-Generated Content

Ashley MacIsaac, a musician from Cape Breton, is suing Google for defamation after the company's AI generated false information about him. This incident highlights the risks of AI-generated content and the need for tech companies to take responsibility for the information they publish.

## The Cost of AI Build-Outs

Stephen O'Grady (RedMonk) has analyzed the costs of AI build-outs for major tech companies. The sums involved are staggering, with companies like Amazon, Alphabet, and Microsoft spending over 50% of their revenues on AI development. Apple is a notable exception, spending around 10% of its revenues on AI.

## The Case for Local Models

Willem van den Ende argues that local models can be a viable alternative to cloud-based models. He notes that the quality of a harness (coding agent + skills + extensions) can matter as much as the model itself. Van den Ende describes his setup for local model work, which includes sandboxing with Nono and using open, local models.

## The Potential for Local Models to Disrupt the Cloud AI Model Game

Nate B Jones argues that Apple's decision to focus on local models could be a strategic move to disrupt the cloud AI model game. He notes that Apple's approach could lead to a future where local models become the norm, and cloud-based models are no longer necessary.

## The Tar Pit of Large-System Programming

Fred Brooks' classic book "The Mythical Man-Month" describes the challenges of large-system programming as a tar pit, where teams become stuck and unable to make progress. Kent Beck's recent post updates this imagery to the "Genie Tarpit," where AI tools struggle to produce software with internal quality.

## The Question of Internal Quality in Agentic Programming

Beck's post raises the question of whether internal quality matters in the age of agentic programming. Some argue that internal quality is essential for building maintainable software, while others believe that AI tools can overcome the limitations of poor internal quality. The answer to this question remains an open one.
