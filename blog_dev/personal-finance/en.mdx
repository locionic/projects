# What I've Actually Learned About Side Hustles and Passive Income

Every personal finance blog tells you the same thing: build a side hustle, reinvest into passive income, retire early. I've been working on this for a few years and the reality is messier — some things worked, some were a waste of time, and a lot of the "passive income" talk is marketing.

Here's my honest experience.

## Freelancing Worked (With a Catch)

Freelancing as a developer was the fastest way to generate meaningful side income. I charged $50-80/hour on Upwork and later through direct referrals. The money was good — $1,000-2,000/month reasonably consistently.

The catch: it's not passive at all. Every dollar you earn trades directly for an hour of your time. If you stop, the income stops. And after a full day of coding for your day job, freelancing means more coding in the evenings. I burned out after six months of doing both.

What I'd do differently: limit freelancing to 10 hours per week max. Any more and the quality of both your main job and your side work drops.

## Digital Products: Low Maintenance, Low Returns

I sell a small collection of developer templates and utilities on Gumroad. One is a Next.js starter kit I built for my own use and cleaned up for sale. It makes about $100/month on average — not life-changing, but it's been earning for two years with maybe 10 hours of total maintenance.

The myth of "build once, sell infinitely" is half true. You do build once, but you spend more time on marketing, documentation, support emails, and payment tax forms than you expect. The ratio is about 60% building, 40% everything else.

## What I Stopped Believing

- **"Anyone can build passive income."** Not really. Digital products require a skill you can productize. Real estate requires capital. Dividend investing requires large upfront investment. The people making passive income work are usually leveraging something they already have — skills, money, or audience.
- **"Start small and scale."** This works for freelancing but not for products. A $5 template sells the same way a $50 template does — marketing is the bottleneck, not price.
- **"Reinvest everything."** I tried this and ended up with a portfolio of half-finished projects. Now I keep 50% of side income as cash and reinvest the rest. Better to have one finished product than five started ones.

<Callout type="info" title="The Numbers Framework That Works for Me">
- 50% of side income → savings/buffer
- 30% → tools and project costs
- 20% → fun (because side work without any reward is just more work)
</Callout>

## What I'd Tell Someone Starting Out

If you're a developer looking to make extra money: start with freelancing for 5-10 hours a week. Build a few small tools for yourself along the way. If one of them happens to be useful to other people, clean it up and put a price on it. If it doesn't sell, that's fine — you needed the tool anyway.

Most passive income advice is written by people who sell courses about passive income. The real version is slower, smaller, and involves more spreadsheet math than you'd expect. But the small, real version has been more sustainable for me than chasing the big, mythical one ever was.
