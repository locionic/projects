I used to think “IaaS, PaaS, SaaS” was just alphabet soup. Then I tried to explain it to someone and realized the only thing that matters is this: who’s responsible for the boring stuff.

The fast way to remember the tiers is simple: you’re renting different layers of the stack.

### IaaS = you rent the computer
IaaS (Infrastructure as a Service) means renting raw computing power. You get servers, storage, and networking—like a virtual machine you can log into.

But the provider isn’t managing your operating system or your application environment. You’re handling the OS, middleware, your app, and the scaling strategy. If something breaks in the “your stack” layer, that’s on you.

This is the choice when you want maximum control and your team is comfortable operating systems and infrastructure. If you don’t want to manage that level of detail, IaaS can feel like you signed up to build the house instead of just living in it.

### PaaS = you rent the place where apps run
PaaS (Platform as a Service) skips a chunk of the infrastructure hassle. You rent a ready-to-code platform, not just servers.

You still deploy your code, but the provider handles a lot behind the scenes: runtime environments, scaling, and the underlying infrastructure plumbing. You focus on the app itself—how it behaves, how it’s built, how it delivers value.

The tradeoff is that you’re working within the platform’s boundaries. It’s less “anything goes” than IaaS, but you’re also spending way less time herding servers.

### SaaS = you rent the finished product
SaaS (Software as a Service) is the layer furthest from the infrastructure. You don’t provision servers. You don’t configure runtimes. You just use the software through a browser or an app.

Tools like email, file storage, or CRM systems are classic SaaS examples. You’re configuring the product to fit your needs, not designing the platform underneath it.

SaaS is great when you want to get work done without building or operating anything. You’re trading some flexibility for speed and simplicity.

### Which one should you pick?
Pick based on how much control you want and how much operational responsibility you’re willing to own.

If you want to control everything down to the server and software environment, go IaaS. If you want to build and ship faster without managing the full stack, go PaaS. If you just need the tool and don’t want to run infrastructure at all, go SaaS.

It depends on your situation, and that’s the annoying part. But once you see the “who manages what” responsibility shift, the acronyms stop feeling like trivia and start feeling obvious.
