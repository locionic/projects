# Using Google Search Console to Grow Your Blog

Setting up Google Search Console is easy — add a TXT record to your domain, submit your sitemap, and you're done. Actually reading the data and deciding what to do next is the hard part.

Here's what I look at and how I act on it.

## The Performance Report

The Performance tab is where most of the actionable data lives. It shows impressions, clicks, CTR, and average position for every query Google sends traffic for.

**Look for pages with high impressions but low CTR.** If a page shows up in search results often but people don't click it, the title and meta description aren't compelling enough. Rewriting them is a 10-minute fix that often doubles click-through.

**Look for queries where your position is 5-15.** These are "striking distance" keywords — close enough to the top of page 1 that a targeted update (addressing the query explicitly in your content) can push you into the top 3 positions.

## Request Indexing for Every New Post

After publishing, paste the URL into GSC's URL Inspection tool and click "Request Indexing." This bypasses the normal crawl queue and tells Google to index your page immediately. Without this, it can take days to weeks for Google to discover a new post.

I do this for every post and see it indexed within 24 hours consistently.

## The Coverage Report Catches Mistakes

The Coverage tab shows which pages Google has indexed and which it couldn't. The most common issues I've seen:

- **404 errors** — I renamed a slug and forgot to update internal links
- **Pages blocked by robots.txt** — Happens when testing staging changes
- **Soft 404s** — Pages that return 200 but have no content (usually a bug)
- **Duplicate without canonical** — Multiple URLs serving the same content

Fixing these is usually straightforward once you know they exist. The problem is most people never check.

<Callout type="info" title="Set a Weekly Reminder">
I review GSC data once a week for 15 minutes. The routine: check for new Coverage errors, look at the top 10 queries by impressions to see if any CTR has dropped, and skim the "new" queries to spot content opportunities.
</Callout>

## Finding Content Ideas From Search Data

The most underused feature of GSC is finding what people are searching for that you haven't written about. In the Performance report, filter by "queries" and look at the bottom of the list —long-tail queries where you show up at position 20+. Each one is a potential blog post topic.

If people find your site for "fix ubuntu login delay part 2" and your post about that topic is ranking well, write a post about the related topic they're also searching for. Write for what your readers are already telling you they need.
