# When DNSSEC Goes Wrong: Lessons from the .de TLD Outage

On May 5, 2026, at approximately 19:30 UTC, DENIC, the registry operator for the .de country-code top-level domain (TLD), began publishing incorrect DNSSEC signatures for the .de zone. This misconfiguration had a ripple effect, causing validating DNS resolvers, including Cloudflare's 1.1.1.1, to reject the signatures and return SERVFAIL to clients.

## The Impact of the Outage

The .de TLD is one of the largest on the Internet, and an outage at this level of the DNS hierarchy has the potential to make millions of domains unreachable. The incident affected not only .de domains but also other TLDs that rely on the .de zone for DNS resolution.

## How DNSSEC Works

DNSSEC (Domain Name System Security Extensions) adds cryptographic authentication to DNS, ensuring the integrity of DNS records. When a zone is signed with DNSSEC, each set of records is accompanied by a digital signature (RRSIG record) that allows a resolver to verify the records haven't been tampered with.

DNSSEC is built on a chain of trust, starting at the root zone, whose trust anchor is hard-coded into resolvers. Each zone delegates trust to child zones via Delegation Signer (DS) records. A break in this chain causes validation to fail for everything below it.

## The Cause of the Outage

The outage was caused by a misconfiguration during a routine key rollover process. DENIC generated and distributed non-validatable signatures, which were rejected by validating resolvers.

## Mitigations Applied by Cloudflare

While the issue was largely out of Cloudflare's control, the company applied temporary mitigations to reduce the impact of the outage.

### Serve Stale

Cloudflare's 1.1.1.1 resolver implements "serve stale," a behavior that allows the resolver to continue serving expired cached records rather than returning an error. This significantly cushions the impact of an upstream outage, buying time for operators to respond.

### Negative Trust Anchors

Cloudflare deployed an override rule mechanism to mark .de as an insecure zone, effectively bypassing DNSSEC validation for .de queries. This is equivalent to a Negative Trust Anchor (NTA), which is a formalized exception in the DNSSEC specification.

## Lessons Learned

This incident highlights the importance of careful key management and the need for robust testing and validation processes. It also underscores the value of collaboration and communication between DNS operators and the broader community.

### Takeaways

* DNSSEC misconfigurations can have far-reaching consequences, affecting millions of domains.
* The chain of trust is critical to DNSSEC's integrity, and breaks in this chain can cause validation to fail.
* Temporary mitigations, such as serve stale and Negative Trust Anchors, can help reduce the impact of an outage.
* Collaboration and communication between DNS operators are essential for resolving incidents and improving the overall security and reliability of the DNS ecosystem.
